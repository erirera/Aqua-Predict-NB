PROPOSAL 3: Groundwater Level Prediction for NB Private Wells

LSTM Forecasting with Drought-Stress and Water Quality Risk Integration

3.1 Background & Motivation

Over 60% of New Brunswick's population relies on groundwater for drinking water — including more than 100,000 private well users and 55 municipal well fields. Yet NB has no AI-driven groundwater level forecasting system. The NB Online Well Log System (OWLS) has accumulated over 30 years of water well records since 1994, providing a rich, province-wide time series dataset that has never been used for machine learning-based prediction.

Critically, NB's fractured bedrock aquifers — the dominant aquifer type in the Appalachian zone covering most of the province — are particularly vulnerable to seasonal drought and long-term climate-driven recharge reduction. Private well owners have no tool to anticipate whether their well will run dry during a drought year. This research directly addresses that gap.

3.2 Research Objectives

  • Build an LSTM-based groundwater level (GWL) forecasting model trained on NB OWLS well records and ECCC meteorological drivers.
  • Forecast GWL 1–6 months ahead at individual wells, with prediction intervals quantifying drought-season risk.
  • Identify NB wells at highest risk of seasonal water table drop below the pump intake depth.
  • Overlay AI-predicted low-GWL zones with the NB Groundwater Chemistry Atlas to flag wells where drought-induced concentration of arsenic, uranium, or methane becomes a public health concern.
  • Produce a public-facing interactive risk map for NB private well owners.

3.3 Data Sources

  • NB OWLS Database: Water well records since 1994 (depth, yield, quality).
  • ECCC Historical Climate: Monthly precipitation, temperature.
  • ERA5-Land (ECMWF): Gap-fill climate data.
  • NB Surficial Geology: Quaternary deposit controls.
  • NB Groundwater Chemistry Atlas: Arsenic, uranium, methane risk zones.
  • CMIP6 SSP2-4.5 (PCIC): Climate projections for NB to 2050.

3.5 Solo Researcher Feasibility

  • Python Hub: Pandas, NumPy, PyTorch (LSTM), scikit-learn.
  • Compute: Standard laptop CPU — LSTM training on tabular time series is fast.
  • Cost: $0 — all datasets are open access.
  • Duration: 5–7 months (Recommended first project).
  • Impact: Direct translation to public health forecasting dashboard.

3.4 Methodology

Phase 1 — Dataset Construction (Months 1–2)

Extract OWLS wells with >= 5 years of records, align with ECCC climate data, compute 1/3/6-month lagged features, define 'dry risk' flag.

Phase 2 — Model Development (Months 2–4)

Train province-wide LSTM encoder-decoder with static covariates. Quantify uncertainty using quantile regression LSTM for 10th/50th/90th percentile forecasts.

Phase 3 — Risk Integration & Output (Months 4–6)

Identify high-drought-risk wells, spatially join with Groundwater Chemistry Atlas, build interactive Streamlit web app.
